Understanding Nutrition
& Your Body

Learn the science behind what you eat — from macronutrients to micronutrients — and how they power every function in your body.

🥦🍎🥜🐟🌾

What is Nutrition?

Nutrition is the process of consuming and using food to support the body's growth, energy, and overall health. A healthy diet helps maintain body functions, strengthens immunity, and reduces the risk of disease.

Food provides your body with the raw materials it needs to build cells, produce energy, regulate hormones, and carry out thousands of biochemical reactions every second. The quality and balance of your diet directly affects how you feel, think, and perform.

Key Insight: "You are what you eat" isn't just a saying — the nutrients in your food literally become the building blocks of your body's tissues, enzymes, and hormones.

Macronutrients

Carbohydrates, Proteins, and Fats are macronutrients — they provide energy and support body structure. They are needed in relatively large amounts daily.

Micronutrients

Vitamins and Minerals are micronutrients — they help regulate body processes, support immunity, and enable chemical reactions. They are needed in smaller amounts but are critically important.

Nutritional Balance & Requirements

To stay healthy, our body needs a proper balance of all nutrients. No single food or nutrient is enough on its own — a healthy diet comes from combining carbohydrates, proteins, fats, vitamins, minerals, and water in the right proportions.

When nutrients are balanced, the body gets enough energy, grows properly, and functions efficiently. An imbalance, whether too little or too much, can lead to weakness, health issues, or disease.

Remember: Eating healthy is about the right combination and quantities, not just eating "clean foods." Even nutritious foods can cause problems when consumed in wrong proportions.

RDA, AI, AMDR & UL

Nutrition guidelines provide clear recommendations on how much of each nutrient we should consume. Here are the key reference values:

RDA — Recommended Dietary Allowance

The average daily amount of a nutrient needed to meet the requirements of most healthy people (covering ~97–98% of the population).

AI — Adequate Intake

A recommended intake level used when there is not enough evidence to set an RDA. Assumed to be sufficient for health based on observed intakes.

AMDR — Acceptable Macronutrient Distribution Range

The recommended range of carbohydrates, proteins, and fats as a percentage of total daily calories for good health.

UL — Upper Limit (Tolerable Upper Intake Level)

The maximum amount of a nutrient that can be consumed daily without causing harmful effects. Exceeding the UL increases the risk of adverse health effects.

Carbohydrates

🌾🍞🍚

Carbohydrates are one of the main nutrients that give your body energy. They are the body's primary source of fuel, helping you stay active and focused throughout the day. Foods like rice, bread, fruits, and potatoes are rich in carbohydrates.

When you eat carbs, your body breaks them down into glucose (sugar), which is used for energy. Eating the right amount of healthy carbohydrates is important for maintaining energy levels and overall health.

Types of Carbohydrates

There are three main types of carbohydrates:

Sugars (Simple Carbohydrates)

Basic form of carbs. Found in fruits, milk, sweets, desserts, and soft drinks. They digest quickly and provide fast energy.

Starches (Complex Carbohydrates)

Made of many sugar units. Found in rice, bread, pasta, potatoes, and corn. They give energy after breaking down into sugar slowly.

Fiber (Complex Carbohydrate)

Not fully digested by the body. Found in fruits, vegetables, nuts, seeds, and whole grains. Helps digestion and keeps you full longer.

Simple Carbohydrates

Simple carbohydrates are sugars that are quickly digested and give fast energy. They are found in foods like fruits, milk, sugar, sweets, and soft drinks. While they provide immediate energy, overconsumption can lead to blood sugar spikes and weight gain.

Complex Carbohydrates

Complex carbohydrates are made of many sugar units and are digested slowly, giving steady energy. They are found in foods like rice, whole grains, bread, pasta, potatoes, and vegetables. They are preferred for sustained energy and better satiety.

Energy from Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates provide 4 kcal per gram of energy — the same as protein, making them an efficient fuel source for the brain and muscles.

Proteins

🥚🥩🐟

Proteins are the building blocks of the body. They are made up of amino acids and are essential for growth, repair, and maintenance of all body tissues — from muscles and skin to organs and enzymes.

When you eat protein, your body breaks it down into amino acids that are used to rebuild cells, produce hormones, and support immune function. Getting enough protein is critical at every stage of life.

Types of Proteins

Proteins are classified based on their amino acid profile:

Complete Proteins

Contain all 9 essential amino acids. Found in animal sources like eggs, meat, fish, and dairy. Best for muscle building and repair.

Incomplete Proteins

Lack one or more essential amino acids. Found in plant sources like lentils, beans, nuts, and grains. Combine multiple sources for completeness.

Complementary Proteins

Two incomplete proteins combined to form a complete amino acid profile. Example: rice + lentils (dal-chawal) — a classic complete protein meal.

Complete Proteins

Complete proteins provide all essential amino acids in adequate amounts. They are the most efficient for building and repairing body tissues. Best sources include eggs, chicken breast, fish, Greek yogurt, and paneer.

Incomplete Proteins

Incomplete proteins are still valuable — combining them throughout the day ensures your body gets all the amino acids it needs. Plant-based diets can be protein-complete with careful food pairing: legumes + grains, or nuts + seeds.

Energy from Proteins

Proteins provide 4 kcal per gram of energy — equal to carbohydrates. However, protein's primary role is structural, not fuel. The body uses protein for energy only when carbohydrate and fat stores are insufficient.

Requirement per Day: Sedentary adults need about 0.8g per kg of body weight. Active individuals, athletes, or those building muscle need 1.2–2.0g per kg. According to AMDR, protein should make up 10–35% of total daily calories.

Fats

🥑🫒🥜

Dietary fats are essential macronutrients that the body needs for energy, hormone production, and absorbing fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K). They also protect organs and keep the body warm.

Not all fats are equal — healthy fats like those in avocados, olive oil, and nuts are beneficial, while trans fats found in processed foods should be strictly avoided. Choosing the right types of fat is key to heart and metabolic health.

Types of Fats

There are three main categories of dietary fats:

Unsaturated Fats (Healthy)

Found in avocados, olive oil, nuts, seeds, and fatty fish. Support heart health, reduce inflammation, and lower bad cholesterol. Should make up most of your fat intake.

Saturated Fats (Limit)

Found in butter, coconut oil, red meat, and full-fat dairy. Raise LDL cholesterol when consumed in excess. Limit to less than 10% of total daily calories.

Trans Fats (Avoid)

Found in processed foods, fried items, and baked goods. Increase LDL (bad) cholesterol and lower HDL (good) cholesterol. WHO recommends eliminating them from diets.

Unsaturated Fats

Unsaturated fats — both monounsaturated (olive oil, avocado) and polyunsaturated (omega-3s in fish, flaxseed) — are heart-healthy fats that actively reduce the risk of cardiovascular disease and support brain function.

Saturated Fats

Saturated fats are solid at room temperature (butter, ghee, coconut oil). In moderate amounts they are acceptable, but excess consumption is associated with increased risk of heart disease and elevated cholesterol levels.

Energy from Fats

Fats provide 9 kcal per gram of energy — more than double that of carbohydrates or protein, making them the most energy-dense macronutrient. They serve as the body's primary long-term energy storage.

Vitamins & Minerals

Vitamins and minerals are micronutrients that your body needs in small amounts to function properly. They don't provide energy directly but are essential for hundreds of processes — from immune defense to bone formation.

Key Vitamins

  • Vitamin A: Vision, immune function, skin health (carrots, sweet potato)
  • Vitamin C: Immunity, collagen production, antioxidant (citrus, guava)
  • Vitamin D: Calcium absorption, bone health (sunlight, fish, eggs)
  • Vitamin B12: Nerve function, red blood cell formation (meat, dairy)
  • Folate (B9): Cell growth, especially during pregnancy (leafy greens, lentils)

Key Minerals

  • Calcium: Bone and teeth strength (dairy, tofu, ragi)
  • Iron: Oxygen transport in blood (spinach, red meat, lentils)
  • Magnesium: Muscle & nerve function (nuts, seeds, whole grains)
  • Zinc: Immunity, wound healing (pumpkin seeds, shellfish, meat)
  • Potassium: Blood pressure regulation (banana, potato, coconut water)

Tip: A diet rich in a variety of colourful vegetables, whole grains, legumes, nuts, and lean proteins naturally provides most essential vitamins and minerals without the need for supplements.

Body Metrics

Body metrics help you understand your physical health using simple calculations based on your height, weight, age, and gender. These metrics are widely used to assess fitness, fat levels, and daily energy needs.

1. Body Mass Index (BMI)

BMI is a measure that uses your height and weight to estimate whether your body weight is healthy.

Formula: BMI = Weight (kg) / Height (m²)

  • Underweight: BMI < 18.5
  • Normal: 18.5 – 24.9
  • Overweight: 25 – 29.9
  • Obese: 30+

2. Body Fat Percentage

Body fat percentage indicates how much of your body weight is made up of fat. It gives a more accurate picture than BMI.

  • Men: Healthy range → 10–20%
  • Women: Healthy range → 18–28%

Note: Women naturally have higher body fat due to hormonal and reproductive needs.

3. Daily Calorie Requirement

This tells how many calories your body needs per day based on metabolism and activity level.

Depends on: Age, gender, weight, height, and activity level.
